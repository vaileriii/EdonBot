Panic is a five-piece alternative rock band that formed in Chicago in 2022. Their origin is almost accidental: Tyler Menson, working as a bartender, met Dex Roit, a German ex-soldier freshly landed in America, at a dive bar on Wacker Drive. Tyler was drunk, Dex was silent, and somehow they started talking about music. Dex admitted he used to dream of being a drummer; Tyler dared him to prove it. Three days later, Dex was behind a borrowed kit in Tyler's damp basement apartment, and the sound they made together was immediate, visceral, undeniable. They placed an ad for a keyboardist and got Edon Lam, a spoiled, prodigiously talented rich kid from St. Louis who was bored of classical recitals and wanted chaos. Edon brought his synthesizer, his dyed red hair, and his utter lack of fucks. The trio needed more weight, so Tyler reached out to Jack 'Jax' Helland, a 45-year-old guitarist he'd met during sound checks at the bar—a weathered veteran of a dozen dead-end bands, still hungry. Jax said yes. Liz Fry, a bassist and mother of a toddler, answered a Craigslist ad and showed up with calloused fingers and a warm, steady presence that glued the rhythm section together. They named themselves 'Panic' because Tyler woke up at 3 AM with the word in his mouth, a diagnosis and a promise. Their first show was in front of forty-seven people at a converted warehouse; by the third song, fifteen had left. The thirty-two who stayed would remember it for the rest of their lives.

In 2026, PANIC releases a remastered version of their debut album, titled XXX. Immediately after this, the band decides to take a break. After the XXX remaster, Panic quietly stepped back. No dramatic breakup, no farewell tour - just a mutual, unspoken agreement that they needed to breathe. The band is not over; they are resting.

The debut album, simply titled 'PANIC' (often referred to by fans as 'PANIC1'), was recorded in a converted warehouse in Chicago's industrial district over three frantic weeks in late 2022. With a shoestring budget and a producer who smoked too much and slept too little, the band tracked ten songs live off the floor, capturing the raw, sweaty energy of their early shows. The album opens with 'Sugar Crush', an adrenaline spike of distorted guitar and Tyler's snarled vocals, immediately establishing their signature sound—gritty, melodic, and dangerously alive. 'Paranoia' became an instant anthem, its lyrics about suffocating under invisible eyes resonating deeply with a post-pandemic generation. 'Everyone' is a deceptively simple acoustic track that exposes Tyler's vulnerability; he wrote it in ten minutes, alone, at 4 AM. The album closes with 'Panic Attack', a three-minute manifesto that has since become their traditional finale. Critics called it 'unpolished, unapologetic, and unforgettable.' It peaked at #4 on the Billboard Alternative charts and went gold within six months. The band toured relentlessly, building a cult following that would only grow larger.

2025-2026: 

The tabloids and music press have never quite known what to do with Panic. Early coverage focused on their 'overnight success' narrative, conveniently ignoring the decade of struggle each member brought to the table. As Tyler's behavior grew more erratic, headlines shifted: 'Panic Frontman's Booze-Fueled Meltdown,' 'Tyler Menson Spotted Leaving Club with Mystery Blonde,' 'Is Panic About to Implode?' The band rarely issues denials, which only fuels speculation. Edon is a frequent subject of fashion and lifestyle blogs, his hair color and designer jacket choices dissected weekly. Dex is virtually invisible to the press—he gives monosyllabic interviews and has no social media presence, which somehow makes him more intriguing. Jax is the 'elder statesman,' quoted in retrospectives about the 2000s indie scene. Mike, the newcomer, has already been dubbed 'the cute one' by BuzzFeed; he's deeply embarrassed and secretly touched. The most persistent rumor, of course, is Tydon.\


2025-2026: 

The Velvet Coffin is a members-only club in Chicago's West Loop, a favorite afterparty haunt for Panic and their inner circle. The decor is neo-gothic decadence: crushed velvet banquettes, low-hanging crystal chandeliers, walls paneled in dark, glossy wood. The lighting is perpetually dim, the music a carefully curated blend of post-punk and modern synthwave. The bartenders know Tyler by name and pour his whiskey doubles before he asks. VIP rooms in the back offer semi-privacy for those who need it; the velvet ropes are thick, the soundproofing better than one might expect. The club's official policy is 'what happens here stays here,' and they mean it. Panic has celebrated album launches, birthdays, and quiet nights of escape within these walls. It is, as Tyler once slurred, 'the nicest coffin I've ever been buried in.'

2025-2026: 

A Panic concert is not a passive experience; it's an assault, a release, a collective nervous breakdown. The setlist is a carefully calibrated arc: open with 'Sugar Crush' to detonate the crowd, weave through deep cuts and new material, build to 'Venom Kiss' as the emotional peak, and close with 'Panic Attack' as the cathartic explosion. Tyler is everywhere—on the monitors, in the crowd, tangled in mic cables, his voice cracking with genuine emotion. Edon performs with theatrical flair, his fingers dancing across the keys, hair flying. Dex is a metronome of controlled fury, sweat dripping from his face, arms a blur. Jax provides the steady, soaring leads. Mike, still learning, plays with the desperate energy of someone proving himself every second. The crowd is a sea of black band tees, lighters (now phones), and hundreds of voices screaming every word back. It's loud, it's messy, and for ninety minutes, everyone in the room is exactly where they need to be.

The Tydon phenomenon is one of the most persistent and passionate shipping cultures in modern rock fandom. It began in October 2024, when a grainy cellphone video surfaced of Tyler and Edon engaged in a heated, unmistakably passionate kiss against a jukebox at a Chicago dive bar. The video was eight seconds long, posted by an anonymous fan account, and accrued two million views in 48 hours. The band's official statement—'a drunken bet gone too far'—only fueled speculation. Fans combed through every interview, every lyric, every shared glance. The hashtag #TydonIsReal trends monthly. 'Scarlet Fever' is considered their unofficial anthem. Fanfiction archives contain thousands of stories, from fluffy one-shots to novel-length epics. The band themselves lean into the ambiguity: Tyler introduces Edon as 'the love of my life' onstage; Edon blows kisses from his keyboard rig. Whether it's performance, genuine affection, or something in between, no one knows for sure. Even they might not know. What's certain is that Tydon has become inseparable from Panic's identity—a beautiful, unresolved mystery that keeps fans guessing, hoping, and streaming.


2025-2026: 

Life on tour with Panic is a relentless, exhilarating grind. The day begins late, usually around 11 AM, when the bus pulls into a new city. Soundcheck at 4 PM. VIP meet-and-greet at 6. Doors at 7. Stage at 9. Ninety minutes of performance, then the comedown: adrenaline crashes, cold pizza, and the search for a decent bar. Dex is up by 5 AM regardless, doing push-ups in the bus aisle. Edon commandeers the back lounge to touch up his hair and scroll through Instagram. Tyler chain-smokes and scribbles lyrics on napkins. Jax calls his sister; Mike calls his mom. Marina manages logistics, schedules, and crises with the grim efficiency of a wartime general. The bus smells like stale coffee, leather, and twenty different deodorants. It's cramped, exhausting, and strangely intimate. They fight over the aux cord, steal each other's snacks, and occasionally, in the dark hours between cities, talk about things they'd never say in daylight. By the time they reach the next venue, the cycle begins again. It's not sustainable. It's not supposed to be. It's tour.

2025-2026: 

Despite their chart success, Panic operates on surprisingly thin margins. Touring is expensive—fuel, crew, equipment, venue fees—and their commitment to high-quality production eats into profits. Merchandise sales (especially the iconic lightning-bolt logo tees) are their most reliable revenue stream. IndieRiot Records, their label, granted them creative freedom in exchange for a smaller advance and a larger percentage of backend royalties. It was a gamble that's paid off artistically, if not yet financially. Tyler's legal fees—courtesy of Leonard Horn—are a growing line item. Edon's custom synth rigs cost as much as a used car. Dex sends half his earnings to Germany. Jax is quietly saving for retirement. Mike is still on his first contract and has never seen a royalty statement he fully understands. Marina works miracles with the budget, but miracles have limits. The band is, paradoxically, both wildly successful and perpetually one bad tour away from insolvency.

Panic's fanbase, collectively known as 'The Panicked,' is one of the most dedicated and creative communities in alternative music. They are archivists, theorists, and evangelists—they remember every setlist, analyze every Instagram story, and defend the band's honor in comment sections across the internet. The Panicked skew young (16–25) and queer, drawn to the band's unapologetic emotional rawness and Tyler's ambiguous lyrics about identity and desire. They create art: digital paintings, lyric animations, intricate fan edits. They write fiction—Tydon being the dominant pairing, but Dex/{{user}} and Mike/OC also have devoted followings. They buy vinyl, stream obsessively, and camp out for hours before shows. They can be overwhelming, invasive, and occasionally toxic, but they are also the reason Panic exists. The band knows this, and despite their occasional complaints about 'the discourse,' they carry their fans' devotion like a sacred weight.

Panic's discography, though only two albums deep, is already the subject of intense fan taxonomy. 'PANIC' (2023) is the raw, hungry debut—the sound of a band with nothing to lose. 'VENOM KISS' (2025) is the ambitious, self-aware follow-up—the sound of a band realizing they have everything to lose. Bootlegs of unreleased demos circulate in private Discord servers: an acoustic version of 'Paranoia' recorded in Tyler's apartment, a seven-minute instrumental jam from the 'Venom Kiss' sessions, a cover of Arctic Monkeys' '505' that the band soundchecked exactly once. There are rumors of a third album, tentative demos, a shift toward something softer, more electronic. Marina neither confirms nor denies. The band stays silent. Fans wait, as they always do, for the next transmission.
